01
Three Problems, Three Architectural Decisions
How each limitation of LSTM+Attention maps directly to a design choice in the Transformer
▾

Page 3 ended by identifying three problems that LSTM-based attention models cannot fully solve. The Transformer, introduced by Vaswani et al. in "Attention is All You Need" (NeurIPS 2017), addresses each one with a specific architectural decision. Understanding this correspondence — problem → solution — is the clearest way to see why the Transformer is designed the way it is, and to avoid memorising its components as arbitrary choices.

From LSTM+Attention problems to Transformer solutions — a direct correspondence

Notice that removing recurrence creates a new problem: without sequential processing, the model has no inherent sense of word order. The word "dog bites man" and "man bites dog" would be indistinguishable if the model cannot tell which position each word occupies. This is why positional encodings are added — they inject position information as a mathematical signal mixed into the token embeddings, compensating for the loss of order that recurrence provided for free. Every major design decision in the Transformer is motivated this way: one door closes, another design choice is needed to handle what the closed door used to provide implicitly.

✅ The Central Claim of the Transformer Paper
The paper's title — "Attention is All You Need" — is a direct claim that recurrence (LSTM/GRU) is not necessary for sequence modelling. Everything recurrence was doing — capturing context, building representations over time, modelling dependencies — can be replaced by a richer, fully parallel attention mechanism applied directly over the entire sequence at once. The paper proved this empirically on machine translation: a Transformer trained for 3.5 days on 8 GPUs outperformed all previous LSTM-based models that took weeks to train. The computational efficiency gain was as important as the quality gain.
02
Self-Attention — Every Token Queries Every Other Token
How attending within a single sequence builds rich contextualised representations without any recurrence
▾

In Page 3, attention meant the decoder querying the encoder — a cross-sequence operation. Self-attention is a different idea: a sequence attending to itself. Every position in a sequence computes a weighted sum of all other positions in the same sequence, letting each token gather contextual information from every other token simultaneously, in a single operation, without any recurrence.

To see why this is powerful, consider encoding the word "bank" in the sentence "The river bank was steep." Ideally, the encoder's representation of "bank" should capture that it means a geographical feature, not a financial institution — and that information comes from "river", which appears nearby. In an LSTM, "bank" can only receive information from "river" by having it flow through the intermediate hidden states h_1, h_2, h_3, h_4, each of which may have diluted it. In self-attention, "bank" computes an attention weight directly against "river" with no intermediaries — the information arrives in one hop.

More concretely, for a sequence of T tokens with representations x_1, …, x_T (each a vector of dimension d), self-attention computes a new representation z_i for each position i as a weighted sum of all other representations:

Self-attention output — each position attends to all positions (including itself)
$$z_i = \sum_{j=1}^{T} \alpha_{ij}\, \mathbf{x}_j \qquad \text{where} \quad \alpha_{ij} = \frac{\exp(e_{ij})}{\sum_{k=1}^{T} \exp(e_{ik})}$$ $$e_{ij} = \frac{\mathbf{x}_i \cdot \mathbf{x}_j}{\sqrt{d}} \qquad \text{(scaled dot-product score)}$$

This is simply the attention mechanism from Page 3, applied with both the query and the keys/values coming from the same sequence. Every token queries every other token (including itself), attention weights are normalised with softmax, and the output at each position is a weighted sum dominated by the most relevant other positions. The result is a new set of T representations z_1, …, z_T, where each z_i is now informed by the full context of the entire sequence.

🧠 Analogy — A Round-Table Discussion vs a Phone Tree
An LSTM is like a phone tree: information passes from person 1 to person 2 to person 3 to… and by the time it reaches person 20, person 1's original message has been paraphrased fifteen times and may be unrecognisable. Self-attention is a round-table discussion: everyone can hear everyone else directly. Person 1 and person 20 can speak to each other without routing through anyone. Each participant synthesises what they heard from others — weighting the contributions of different speakers by how relevant those contributions are to their own understanding. The round table produces richer individual summaries in one round; the phone tree needs many rounds to propagate the same information, and degrades along the way.

The Complete Self-Attention Computation Over a Sequence

"The river bank was steep" — self-attention weights for position "bank" attending to all other positions

Critically, none of the attention weights shown above are hand-designed — they emerge from the dot products between the word embeddings. The model learns embeddings such that the embedding of "river" has a high dot product with the embedding of "bank" when "bank" is used in a geographical context. The same "bank" word in a financial context — "I went to the bank to deposit money" — would produce different attention weights, with "deposit" and "money" receiving high scores instead. This contextual disambiguation is the fundamental reason self-attention produces better representations than static word embeddings.

03
Queries, Keys, and Values — The Full Scaled Dot-Product Attention
Why raw token embeddings aren't used directly — learned projections that decouple what you ask from what you return
▾

The self-attention in Section 2 used the raw token embeddings x_i as both the queries and the keys. This works, but it conflates two separate roles: what a token is looking for (its query) and what it offers to other tokens (its key and value). The Transformer separates these using three learned linear projections: Q (query), K (key), V (value). Each projects the same input into a different space, each learned for a different purpose.

Three projections — same input X, three learned weight matrices
$$\mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{X}\,\mathbf{W}^Q \quad \mathbf{K} = \mathbf{X}\,\mathbf{W}^K \quad \mathbf{V} = \mathbf{X}\,\mathbf{W}^V$$ $$\text{where } \mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_\text{model}},\quad \mathbf{W}^Q, \mathbf{W}^K \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{model} \times d_k},\quad \mathbf{W}^V \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{model} \times d_v}$$
Scaled dot-product attention — the complete formula in matrix form
$$\text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)\mathbf{V}$$

Let us read this equation from inside out. The product QK⊤ is a T × T matrix where entry (i, j) is the dot product between query i and key j — a raw compatibility score between position i asking "what do I need?" and position j offering "here is what I have". Dividing by √d_k normalises the variance as discussed in Section 5 of Page 3. Softmax over each row produces attention weights that sum to 1 across all T positions. Finally, multiplying by V replaces the keys with the actual content each position wants to contribute — and different tokens can contribute different information than what they advertise in their keys.

🧠 Analogy — Database Query System
Think of the attention mechanism as a soft database lookup. The Query is your search term — what you are looking for. The Keys are the metadata tags on each database entry — what each entry advertises about itself. The Values are the actual content of each entry — what gets returned. Matching is done by comparing your query against all keys (dot products), converting matches to weights (softmax), and returning a weighted blend of all values. The crucial insight is that keys and values can be different — an entry's tag (key) can say "I am about rivers" while its actual content (value) carries its full contextual embedding. The Transformer learns what to advertise and what to return separately.

Why Separate Queries, Keys, and Values?

The separation into Q, K, V gives the model more expressiveness than using raw embeddings directly. Consider position i attending to position j. With separate projections, the model learns: "when I project x_i as a query, what should I be looking for?" and "when I project x_j as a key, what should I advertise?" and "when I project x_j as a value, what content should I deliver once selected?". These are genuinely distinct operations, and decoupling them lets the gradient signal train each projection independently for its specific role. In practice, learned Q, K, V projections consistently outperform raw-embedding attention.

The matrix dimensions in the original Transformer paper are: d_model = 512 (embedding size), d_k = d_v = 64 (projection dimension for one attention head). The T × T attention matrix QK⊤ has computational complexity O(T² · d_k) — quadratic in sequence length. This is the Transformer's main scalability limitation: for sequences of length T = 10,000 (document-level), the attention matrix has 100 million entries. Efficient Transformer variants (Longformer, Reformer, Flash Attention) address this, but for most NLP tasks with sequences up to a few thousand tokens, the quadratic cost is manageable.

QKᵀ attention matrix — rows are queries (targets), columns are keys (sources), darker = higher attention weight

Multi-Head Attention — Attending to Different Things Simultaneously
Why one attention pattern isn't enough — h parallel heads, each learning a different relationship type
▾

Single-head attention produces one attention distribution per position — one set of weights over all other tokens. But natural language is rich with multiple simultaneous relationship types. Consider the sentence "The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired." When resolving the pronoun "it", the model needs to attend to "animal" (syntactic coreference). When understanding "cross the street", it needs to attend to nearby words for verb-argument structure. When processing "tired", it needs to attend to "animal" again for semantic agreement. These are different kinds of relationships that might require looking at different positions with different strengths — and a single attention head must somehow represent all of them simultaneously in one set of weights.

Multi-head attention solves this by running h attention operations in parallel, each with its own independent Q, K, V projections. Each head can specialise: one head might learn syntactic dependencies, another might learn semantic similarity, another might learn positional proximity. After all heads compute their attention outputs, the results are concatenated and projected back to the model dimension. The full computation is:

Multi-head attention — h heads in parallel, concatenated and projected
$$\text{head}_i = \text{Attention}(\mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}_i^Q,\; \mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}_i^K,\; \mathbf{X}\mathbf{W}_i^V)$$ $$\text{MultiHead}(\mathbf{X}) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1, \ldots, \text{head}_h)\,\mathbf{W}^O$$ $$\text{where each } \mathbf{W}_i^Q, \mathbf{W}_i^K \in \mathbb{R}^{d_\text{model} \times d_k},\quad d_k = d_\text{model} / h$$

In the original Transformer, d_model = 512 and h = 8, giving d_k = 64 per head. Crucially, the total number of parameters is the same as a single head with d_k = 512: we have 8 smaller projections instead of 1 large one, and the total computation is similar. But the model now has 8 independent subspaces in which to represent different types of relationships.

Multi-head attention — 8 parallel heads, each computing its own attention over the same input, then concatenated

An important property is that the output shape (T × d_model) is identical to the input shape. This is not a coincidence — it is a deliberate design choice that allows Transformer layers to be stacked: the output of one multi-head attention layer can be fed directly into the next, with the same tensor shape flowing through every layer. Each successive layer refines the representations further, allowing distant tokens to interact over multiple "hops" of attention even though any two tokens can reach each other directly in a single layer.

✅ What Heads Actually Learn — Empirical Findings
Researchers have visualised the attention patterns of individual heads in trained Transformers and found consistent specialisation. Some heads learn syntactic structure — attending across subject-verb pairs regardless of distance. Some learn coreference — attending from pronouns back to their referents. Some learn positional patterns — attending primarily to immediately preceding or following positions. Some heads, especially in larger models, do not have obvious interpretable functions and may be capturing statistical regularities that resist simple description. This emergent specialisation happens without any supervision — the only training signal is language modelling or translation loss.

Positional Encodings — Injecting Order Without Recurrence
Why word order is lost when recurrence is removed, and how sinusoidal functions restore it
▾

Self-attention is permutation-equivariant: if you shuffle the order of the input tokens, the attention weights between token pairs stay the same (because they depend only on the token embeddings, not their positions), and the output representations come out in the same shuffled order. This means self-attention alone has no way to distinguish "dog bites man" from "man bites dog" — the same tokens produce the same attention pattern regardless of order. Recurrence solved this implicitly because the hidden state h_t was literally computed after h_{t−1}, building in a left-to-right ordering. Without recurrence, we must make position information explicit.

The Transformer adds a positional encoding PE(pos) to each token's embedding before feeding it into the first layer. The original paper uses sinusoidal functions of different frequencies — one sine/cosine pair per embedding dimension, with the frequency decreasing as the dimension index increases:

Sinusoidal positional encoding — Vaswani et al. 2017
$$\text{PE}(\text{pos}, 2i) = \sin\!\left(\frac{\text{pos}}{10000^{2i/d_\text{model}}}\right)$$ $$\text{PE}(\text{pos}, 2i+1) = \cos\!\left(\frac{\text{pos}}{10000^{2i/d_\text{model}}}\right)$$

Here pos is the token position (0, 1, 2, …) and i is the dimension index (0, 1, …, d_model/2 − 1). The resulting PE(pos) is a d_model-dimensional vector that is unique for each position. At dimension 0, the sine has a very short period (≈6.3 tokens) — it oscillates rapidly and captures fine-grained local position. At dimension d_model/2 − 1, the period is 10000 × 2π ≈ 62,832 tokens — it captures very large-scale position information. All intermediate dimensions capture positions at intermediate scales. Together, the full d_model-dimensional encoding gives every position a unique "fingerprint" over the full range of possible positions.

Sinusoidal positional encodings — each row is one position's encoding vector. Early dims oscillate fast (local), late dims oscillate slowly (global)

Three key properties make the sinusoidal encoding a good choice. First, it is unique — no two positions produce the same encoding (they are guaranteed distinct by the sine/cosine structure). Second, it is smooth — nearby positions have similar encodings, so the model can generalise to nearby positions it has not seen during training. Third — and most elegantly — the encoding of any relative offset PE(pos + k) can be expressed as a linear function of PE(pos), for any fixed offset k. This means the model can learn to attend to tokens at a specific relative position (e.g., "always attend to the previous token" or "always attend 5 positions forward") using a simple linear transformation. Relative position information is inherently accessible in the linear algebra of sinusoidal encodings.

Modern models often replace sinusoidal encodings with learned positional embeddings — just a learnable matrix of shape (max_seq_len, d_model), one embedding per position, trained end-to-end. These consistently perform slightly better on benchmarks because they are optimised directly for the task, rather than being a fixed mathematical construction. The trade-off is that learned embeddings cannot extrapolate beyond the maximum training sequence length — a model trained with positions 0–512 has never seen position 513. Sinusoidal encodings can in principle extrapolate to any length.

⚠️ Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) — The Modern Standard
Most production LLMs (LLaMA, Mistral, Gemma, and many others) now use RoPE (Rotary Position Embeddings), introduced by Su et al. (2021). Rather than adding a positional vector to the token embedding, RoPE multiplies Q and K by rotation matrices whose angles depend on position. This encodes relative positions directly into the attention score computation — the dot product between query at position m and key at position n naturally depends on m−n, the relative offset. RoPE provides the best of both worlds: it generalises to unseen lengths (with extensions like YaRN) and it gives the attention mechanism explicit relative position awareness. Understanding the sinusoidal encoding above gives you the conceptual foundation to understand RoPE when you encounter it.

The Transformer Encoder Block — LayerNorm, Residuals, and FFN
Wrapping multi-head attention in residual connections and layer normalisation — and what the feedforward sublayer actually does
▾

Multi-head attention is the centrepiece of the Transformer, but it does not stand alone. Each encoder layer wraps the attention computation in two additional operations that are critical for stable, deep training: residual connections and layer normalisation. Together these form the standard "Add & Norm" sublayer. The encoder block also contains a position-wise feedforward network (FFN) after the attention sublayer — also wrapped in Add & Norm. Understanding these additions requires understanding why they are needed, not just that they exist.

Residual Connections — Preserving Gradients Through Depth

You encountered residual connections in the CNN architectures page (ResNet). The motivation is identical here: adding the input directly to the sublayer's output creates a gradient highway. For a sublayer F, the output is x + F(x) rather than just F(x). The gradient of the loss with respect to x becomes ∂L/∂(x + F(x)) = ∂L/∂output × (1 + ∂F/∂x) — the "+1" ensures the gradient always has a direct path back, no matter how small ∂F/∂x becomes. Without residuals, stacking 6 or 12 attention layers would cause gradients to vanish in early layers. With residuals, training reliably works at 12, 24, or even 96 layers.

Layer Normalisation — Why Not Batch Norm?

Layer normalisation normalises across the feature dimension (d_model dimensions) for each token independently, rather than across the batch dimension as batch normalisation does. For sequences, this distinction matters: batch statistics are noisy when sequences vary in length or when batch sizes are small, and batch norm behaves differently during training vs inference (it switches from batch statistics to running statistics). Layer norm uses only the statistics of the current token's d_model-dimensional vector, making it training/inference identical, sequence-length agnostic, and fully parallelisable across the batch. In the original Transformer, layer norm is applied after the residual addition (Post-LN); most modern models use Pre-LN (layer norm applied before the sublayer), which trains more stably.

One encoder block — two sublayers, each with Add & Norm
$$\mathbf{a} = \text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{x} + \text{MultiHead}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x},\mathbf{x})) \qquad \text{(attention sublayer)}$$ $$\mathbf{z} = \text{LayerNorm}(\mathbf{a} + \text{FFN}(\mathbf{a})) \qquad \text{(feedforward sublayer)}$$ $$\text{FFN}(\mathbf{a}) = \max(0,\; \mathbf{a}\,\mathbf{W}_1 + \mathbf{b}_1)\,\mathbf{W}_2 + \mathbf{b}_2 \qquad \text{(two linear layers + ReLU)}$$

The Feedforward Network — What it Actually Does

The FFN applies the same two-layer network independently to each token position. The inner dimension is typically 4× the model dimension: d_ff = 4 × d_model = 2048 in the original paper. This expansion-then-compression structure processes each token's representation through a larger space — often described as the network "thinking" about each position separately. While attention allows tokens to interact with each other, the FFN provides per-token computation with no cross-position mixing. In models that have been studied carefully, the FFN layers appear to store factual associations that the attention layers retrieve — a form of key-value memory where attention heads look up which fact is relevant and the FFN provides the content of that fact.

One encoder block — the complete computation graph with residual and LayerNorm wrapper

With N = 6 encoder layers (as in the original Transformer), the full encoder applies this block six times sequentially, each layer refining the representations produced by the previous one. Early layers tend to learn syntactic features (word boundaries, parts of speech), while later layers tend to learn more semantic features (entity types, coreference). This was confirmed empirically by probing classifiers trained on the frozen representations from each layer.

The Transformer Decoder Block — Masked Attention and Cross-Attention
Why decoding needs two different attention operations — and what the causal mask is for
▾

The decoder block is more complex than the encoder block because the decoder must do two things: attend to what it has already generated (to produce coherent continuations) while also attending to the encoder's output (to extract relevant source information). This requires three sublayers rather than two. The first is masked self-attention, the second is cross-attention (exactly the attention mechanism from Page 3), and the third is the same feedforward network as the encoder. All three are wrapped in Add & Norm.

Masked Self-Attention — Not Peeking at the Future

At training time, the decoder receives the entire target sequence at once (teacher forcing, as in Page 3). But when generating position t, the model must not be allowed to look at positions t+1, t+2, … — that would be cheating. At inference time, those future tokens simply don't exist yet. The solution is a causal mask: before the softmax in the self-attention score computation, set all scores e_{i,j} with j > i to −∞. When softmax is applied, exp(−∞) = 0, so those positions receive exactly zero attention weight. Each position can only attend to positions at or before itself — "masked self-attention" or "causal self-attention".

Masked attention score matrix — upper triangle set to −∞ before softmax
$$\text{MaskedAttn}(\mathbf{Q},\mathbf{K},\mathbf{V}) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}} + \mathbf{M}\right)\mathbf{V}$$ $$\text{where } M_{ij} = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } j \leq i \\ -\infty & \text{if } j > i \end{cases}$$
Causal mask — lower triangle only. Position i can see positions 1…i but not i+1…T

Cross-Attention — Querying the Encoder

After masked self-attention, the decoder's representations are enriched with information from the already-generated prefix. The second sublayer is cross-attention (also called encoder-decoder attention): the Queries come from the decoder's current hidden state, while the Keys and Values come from the encoder's final output. This is exactly the attention mechanism from Page 3, now implemented with learned Q, K, V projections. For each decoder position, it asks: given what I have generated so far, which parts of the source sentence should I focus on right now? The encoder output is fixed once encoding is complete, so the same encoder output (K, V) is reused at every decoder layer and every decoding step.

Cross-attention in the decoder — Q from decoder, K and V from encoder output
$$\text{CrossAttn}(\mathbf{d}, \mathbf{e}) = \text{Attention}(\mathbf{d}\mathbf{W}^Q,\; \mathbf{e}\mathbf{W}^K,\; \mathbf{e}\mathbf{W}^V)$$ $$\text{where } \mathbf{d} \in \mathbb{R}^{T' \times d_\text{model}} \text{ (decoder states)},\quad \mathbf{e} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_\text{model}} \text{ (encoder output)}$$

The full decoder block is therefore: masked self-attention (Add & Norm) → cross-attention (Add & Norm) → FFN (Add & Norm). With N = 6 decoder layers, the decoder is six repetitions of this three-sublayer block. The final decoder layer's output is passed through a linear layer and softmax to produce next-token probabilities.

PyTorch — Transformer from Scratch, Then with nn.Transformer
Scaled dot-product attention, multi-head attention, encoder block, and the full model — every line explained
▾

We will build the Transformer in four stages. Writing each component from scratch forces every equation to become concrete code, and the shapes of every tensor will tell you exactly what is happening. After the from-scratch version, we show the clean production path using PyTorch's built-in nn.Transformer and nn.MultiheadAttention.

Part 1 — Scaled Dot-Product Attention

Python · Scaled dot-product attention — the core building block
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F
import math

def scaled_dot_product_attention(Q, K, V, mask=None):
    """
    Q: (batch, heads, seq_q, d_k)
    K: (batch, heads, seq_k, d_k)
    V: (batch, heads, seq_k, d_v)
    mask: (batch, 1, seq_q, seq_k) or (1, 1, seq_q, seq_k) — optional
    Returns:
      output: (batch, heads, seq_q, d_v)
      weights: (batch, heads, seq_q, seq_k) — for visualisation
    """
    d_k = Q.shape[-1]

    # QKᵀ: (batch, heads, seq_q, seq_k)
    # Each [i,j] entry = dot product between query i and key j
    # .transpose(-2,-1) swaps the last two dims of K
    scores = torch.matmul(Q, K.transpose(-2, -1)) / math.sqrt(d_k)

    # Apply optional mask — set masked positions to -inf so softmax → 0
    # mask == 0 means "ignore this position"
    if mask is not None:
        scores = scores.masked_fill(mask == 0, float('-inf'))

    # Softmax over the key dimension: each query gets a distribution over keys
    weights = F.softmax(scores, dim=-1)        # (batch, heads, seq_q, seq_k)

    # Weighted sum of values
    output = torch.matmul(weights, V)           # (batch, heads, seq_q, d_v)
    return output, weights

Part 2 — Multi-Head Attention Module

Python · Multi-head attention — split into h heads, attend in parallel, concatenate
class MultiHeadAttention(nn.Module):
    """
    d_model: overall model dimension (e.g. 512)
    h:       number of heads (e.g. 8)
    d_k = d_v = d_model // h  (dimension per head, e.g. 64)
    
    The key shape insight: we project d_model → d_model, but then
    reshape to treat it as h separate d_k-dimensional heads. This is
    equivalent to h separate (d_model, d_k) projections but done as
    one (d_model, d_model) projection for efficiency.
    """
    def __init__(self, d_model: int, h: int, dropout: float = 0.1):
        super().__init__()
        assert d_model % h == 0, "d_model must be divisible by h"
        self.d_k    = d_model // h
        self.h      = h
        self.d_model= d_model

        # One matrix each for Q, K, V projections — size (d_model, d_model)
        # These are 'h heads worth' of projections packed together
        self.W_q = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model, bias=False)
        self.W_k = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model, bias=False)
        self.W_v = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model, bias=False)
        self.W_o = nn.Linear(d_model, d_model, bias=False)

        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)

    def split_heads(self, x):
        """Reshape (batch, seq, d_model) → (batch, h, seq, d_k)
        
        Think of it as: the d_model projection is partitioned into h
        equal chunks, one per head. transpose puts heads before seq so
        the attention computation works correctly.
        """
        batch, seq, _ = x.shape
        x = x.view(batch, seq, self.h, self.d_k)   # (B, T, h, d_k)
        return x.transpose(1, 2)                        # (B, h, T, d_k)

    def forward(self, query, key, value, mask=None):
        """
        query: (batch, seq_q, d_model)  ← for self-attention, all three are x
        key:   (batch, seq_k, d_model)  ← for cross-attention, key/value from encoder
        value: (batch, seq_k, d_model)
        mask:  optional causal or padding mask
        """
        # Project all three to d_model, then split into h heads
        Q = self.split_heads(self.W_q(query))  # (B, h, seq_q, d_k)
        K = self.split_heads(self.W_k(key))    # (B, h, seq_k, d_k)
        V = self.split_heads(self.W_v(value))  # (B, h, seq_k, d_k)

        # Run attention over all heads simultaneously (batched matmul)
        # attn_out: (B, h, seq_q, d_k)
        attn_out, _ = scaled_dot_product_attention(Q, K, V, mask)

        # Merge heads: (B, h, seq_q, d_k) → (B, seq_q, d_model)
        # transpose back, then view collapses h * d_k = d_model
        batch, _, seq_q, _ = attn_out.shape
        attn_out = attn_out.transpose(1, 2).contiguous()
        attn_out = attn_out.view(batch, seq_q, self.d_model)

        # Final output projection W_o
        return self.W_o(attn_out)               # (B, seq_q, d_model)

Part 3 — Encoder Block, Positional Encoding, Full Encoder

Python · Positional encoding, FFN, encoder block, full encoder stack
class PositionalEncoding(nn.Module):
    """Sinusoidal positional encoding — added to token embeddings.
    Pre-computes the entire pe matrix up to max_len positions.
    """
    def __init__(self, d_model, max_len=5000, dropout=0.1):
        super().__init__()
        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)

        pe  = torch.zeros(max_len, d_model)                      # (max_len, d_model)
        pos = torch.arange(0, max_len).unsqueeze(1).float()     # (max_len, 1)
        # Denominator: 10000^(2i/d_model) for i = 0, 1, ..., d_model//2 - 1
        div = torch.exp(
            torch.arange(0, d_model, 2).float() *
            (-math.log(10000.0) / d_model)
        )                                                             # (d_model//2,)
        pe[:, 0::2] = torch.sin(pos * div)                         # even dims: sin
        pe[:, 1::2] = torch.cos(pos * div)                         # odd dims: cos

        # Register as buffer: saved with model, moved to GPU with .to(device),
        # but NOT counted as a trainable parameter
        self.register_buffer('pe', pe.unsqueeze(0))              # (1, max_len, d_model)

    def forward(self, x):
        """x: (batch, seq, d_model)"""
        return self.dropout(x + self.pe[:, :x.shape[1]])


class FeedForward(nn.Module):
    """Position-wise FFN: same two-layer network applied to every position.
    d_ff = 4 * d_model in the original paper (expansion then compression).
    """
    def __init__(self, d_model, d_ff, dropout=0.1):
        super().__init__()
        self.net = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(d_model, d_ff),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Dropout(dropout),
            nn.Linear(d_ff, d_model),
        )
    def forward(self, x): return self.net(x)


class EncoderBlock(nn.Module):
    """One encoder layer: self-attention + FFN, each with residual and LayerNorm."""
    def __init__(self, d_model, h, d_ff, dropout=0.1):
        super().__init__()
        self.self_attn = MultiHeadAttention(d_model, h, dropout)
        self.ffn       = FeedForward(d_model, d_ff, dropout)
        self.norm1     = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.norm2     = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.drop      = nn.Dropout(dropout)

    def forward(self, x, src_mask=None):
        # Pre-LN (modern style): normalise BEFORE the sublayer
        # Sublayer 1: self-attention over the source sequence
        x = x + self.drop(self.self_attn.forward(
            self.norm1(x), self.norm1(x), self.norm1(x), src_mask
        ))
        # Sublayer 2: position-wise feedforward
        x = x + self.drop(self.ffn(self.norm2(x)))
        return x


class TransformerEncoder(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, vocab_size, d_model, h, d_ff, N, dropout=0.1):
        super().__init__()
        self.embed = nn.Embedding(vocab_size, d_model)
        self.pe    = PositionalEncoding(d_model, dropout=dropout)
        self.scale = math.sqrt(d_model)  # scale embeddings before adding PE
        self.layers= nn.ModuleList([EncoderBlock(d_model, h, d_ff, dropout)
                                       for _ in range(N)])
        self.norm  = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)

    def forward(self, src, src_mask=None):
        """src: (batch, src_len) — token IDs"""
        x = self.pe(self.embed(src) * self.scale)  # (batch, src_len, d_model)
        for layer in self.layers:
            x = layer.forward(x, src_mask)
        return self.norm(x)                          # final layer norm

Part 4 — Decoder Block, Causal Mask, Full Seq2Seq Transformer

Python · Decoder block, mask utils, complete Transformer Seq2Seq
class DecoderBlock(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, d_model, h, d_ff, dropout=0.1):
        super().__init__()
        self.self_attn  = MultiHeadAttention(d_model, h, dropout)  # masked
        self.cross_attn = MultiHeadAttention(d_model, h, dropout)  # encoder Q
        self.ffn        = FeedForward(d_model, d_ff, dropout)
        self.norm1 = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.norm2 = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.norm3 = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.drop  = nn.Dropout(dropout)

    def forward(self, tgt, enc_out, tgt_mask=None, src_mask=None):
        # 1. Masked self-attention: tgt attends to tgt (causal mask)
        tgt = tgt + self.drop(self.self_attn.forward(
            self.norm1(tgt), self.norm1(tgt), self.norm1(tgt), tgt_mask
        ))
        # 2. Cross-attention: decoder queries encoder output
        #    Q from decoder (norm2(tgt)), K and V from encoder (enc_out)
        tgt = tgt + self.drop(self.cross_attn.forward(
            self.norm2(tgt), enc_out, enc_out, src_mask
        ))
        # 3. FFN
        tgt = tgt + self.drop(self.ffn(self.norm3(tgt)))
        return tgt


def make_causal_mask(seq_len, device):
    """Lower-triangular boolean mask — True (1) = can attend, False (0) = masked.
    Shape: (1, 1, seq_len, seq_len) to broadcast over batch and heads.
    """
    mask = torch.tril(torch.ones(seq_len, seq_len, device=device))
    return mask.unsqueeze(0).unsqueeze(0)   # (1, 1, T, T)

def make_padding_mask(token_ids, pad_idx=0):
    """True where token is NOT padding — shape (batch, 1, 1, seq_len)."""
    return (token_ids != pad_idx).unsqueeze(1).unsqueeze(2)


class TransformerSeq2Seq(nn.Module):
    """Complete Transformer encoder-decoder for sequence-to-sequence tasks."""
    def __init__(self, src_vocab, tgt_vocab, d_model=512, h=8,
                 d_ff=2048, N=6, dropout=0.1):
        super().__init__()
        self.encoder    = TransformerEncoder(src_vocab, d_model, h, d_ff, N, dropout)
        self.tgt_embed  = nn.Embedding(tgt_vocab, d_model)
        self.tgt_pe     = PositionalEncoding(d_model, dropout=dropout)
        self.scale      = math.sqrt(d_model)
        self.dec_layers = nn.ModuleList([DecoderBlock(d_model, h, d_ff, dropout)
                                           for _ in range(N)])
        self.norm_dec   = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.fc_out     = nn.Linear(d_model, tgt_vocab)

    def forward(self, src, tgt):
        """
        src: (batch, src_len) — source token IDs
        tgt: (batch, tgt_len) — target token IDs (teacher-forced, includes SOS)
        Returns: logits (batch, tgt_len, tgt_vocab)
        """
        # Padding masks: don't attend to PAD (idx 0) positions
        src_mask = make_padding_mask(src)                          # (B, 1, 1, src_len)
        tgt_pad_mask = make_padding_mask(tgt)                     # (B, 1, 1, tgt_len)
        tgt_len = tgt.shape[1]
        # Causal mask AND padding mask combined with bitwise AND
        causal = make_causal_mask(tgt_len, tgt.device)             # (1, 1, T, T)
        tgt_mask = tgt_pad_mask & causal                            # (B, 1, tgt_len, tgt_len)

        # Encode source sequence — produces (B, src_len, d_model)
        enc_out = self.encoder.forward(src, src_mask)

        # Embed + PE target sequence
        tgt_x = self.tgt_pe(self.tgt_embed(tgt) * self.scale)    # (B, tgt_len, d_model)

        # Decode: N decoder layers, passing enc_out as K, V for cross-attention
        for layer in self.dec_layers:
            tgt_x = layer.forward(tgt_x, enc_out, tgt_mask, src_mask)

        tgt_x = self.norm_dec(tgt_x)
        return self.fc_out(tgt_x)                                  # (B, tgt_len, vocab)


# ── Training ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
device = torch.device('cuda' if torch.cuda.is_available() else 'cpu')
model  = TransformerSeq2Seq(src_vocab=10000, tgt_vocab=10000).to(device)

# Xavier uniform initialisation for all linear layers — standard practice
for p in model.parameters():
    if p.dim() > 1: nn.init.xavier_uniform_(p)

# Learning rate schedule: warmup steps then inverse sqrt decay
# Original paper: lr = d_model^{-0.5} * min(step^{-0.5}, step * warmup^{-1.5})
optimizer = torch.optim.Adam(model.parameters(), lr=0,
                              betas=(0.9, 0.98), eps=1e-9)
criterion = nn.CrossEntropyLoss(ignore_index=0)  # ignore PAD

def get_lr(step, d_model=512, warmup=4000):
    """Transformer learning rate schedule — essential for stable training."""
    step = max(step, 1)
    return d_model**(-0.5) * min(step**(-0.5), step * warmup**(-1.5))

for step, (src, tgt) in enumerate(train_loader, 1):
    # Update learning rate
    lr = get_lr(step)
    for g in optimizer.param_groups: g['lr'] = lr

    src, tgt = src.to(device), tgt.to(device)
    optimizer.zero_grad()

    # Teacher forcing: tgt[:, :-1] as input, tgt[:, 1:] as targets
    # Input:  [SOS, y1, y2, ..., y_{T'-1}]
    # Target: [y1,  y2, ..., y_{T'-1}, EOS]
    logits = model.forward(src, tgt[:, :-1])               # (B, T'-1, vocab)
    loss   = criterion(logits.reshape(-1, logits.shape[-1]),
                        tgt[:, 1:].reshape(-1))

    loss.backward()
    nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), 1.0)
    optimizer.step()
🔑 Two Training Subtleties Unique to Transformers
The learning rate schedule (warmup then decay) is not optional — it is essential. Unlike LSTMs where Adam with a fixed learning rate usually works, Transformer training diverges without warmup if the initial learning rate is too high. The warmup period lets the model parameters settle from random initialisations before the learning rate reaches its peak. The Xavier uniform initialisation for all linear layers is equally important: it sets the initial variance of each layer's weights to preserve the variance of the signal across the depth of the network. Without it, the very deep Transformer (12+ layers) suffers from exploding or vanishing activations at the start of training, even before the first update.

BERT, GPT, and Scale — What You Now Understand
Encoder-only, decoder-only, and the scaling laws that turned Transformers into the foundation of modern AI
▾

The encoder-decoder Transformer from the original paper is a general architecture for mapping one sequence to another. But in practice, many important tasks do not need both halves. Classification tasks — sentiment analysis, question answering, named entity recognition — need a rich encoder but no generation step. Language modelling — predicting the next token to generate text — needs a decoder but no separate encoder to condition on. These observations led to the two dominant Transformer variants: BERT (encoder-only) and GPT (decoder-only).

BERT — Encoder-Only, Bidirectional, Masked Language Modelling

BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers, Devlin et al. 2019) uses only the encoder stack with one critical modification: because there is no autoregressive generation, the causal mask is removed entirely. Every position attends to every other position in both directions — left and right. This bidirectional attention is a significant strength for understanding tasks: when building a representation of the word "bank", BERT can simultaneously look at both "river" (to its left) and "steep" (to its right), producing a richer context-aware embedding than a unidirectional model could.

BERT is trained with two self-supervised objectives. In Masked Language Modelling (MLM), 15% of input tokens are randomly replaced with a [MASK] token, and the model must predict the original token at each masked position using context from both directions. This forces the encoder to build a deep bidirectional understanding of every token. The second objective, Next Sentence Prediction (NSP), trains the model to distinguish pairs of consecutive sentences from random sentence pairs — less important in practice, and often dropped in later work.

GPT — Decoder-Only, Causal, Autoregressive Language Modelling

GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer, Radford et al. 2018) uses only the decoder stack — specifically, the masked self-attention layers — without any encoder or cross-attention. The causal mask ensures each position attends only to preceding positions, making the model inherently autoregressive: it predicts token t+1 given tokens 1…t. Training objective is simply next-token prediction (standard language modelling loss) over a very large text corpus. At inference, the model generates text by sampling or argmax-selecting one token at a time and appending it to the context for the next step.

The GPT approach scales remarkably well. GPT-2 (2019, 1.5B parameters) could generate coherent long-form text. GPT-3 (2020, 175B parameters) demonstrated in-context learning — the ability to perform new tasks given only a few examples in the prompt, without any gradient updates. GPT-4 (2023, undisclosed but estimated >1T parameters) powers production AI assistants. The jump from "language model" to "general-purpose assistant" came almost entirely from scale, instruction fine-tuning (RLHF), and better data curation — the core Transformer architecture barely changed.

Scaling Laws — Why Bigger Models Are Better (and by How Much)

Kaplan et al. (2020) at OpenAI discovered that Transformer language model loss follows smooth power laws with respect to three quantities: number of parameters N, training data size D, and compute budget C. Within a wide range, each quantity can be increased independently and the improvement is predictable: doubling parameters reduces loss by roughly the same amount regardless of the current model size. This is a striking result — it means you can forecast model capability from training compute before training begins.

Neural scaling law — test loss as a power law in parameters, data, and compute
$$L(N) \approx \left(\frac{N_c}{N}\right)^{\alpha_N}, \quad L(D) \approx \left(\frac{D_c}{D}\right)^{\alpha_D}, \quad L(C) \approx \left(\frac{C_c}{C}\right)^{\alpha_C}$$ $$\text{Kaplan et al.: } \alpha_N \approx 0.076, \;\; \alpha_D \approx 0.095, \;\; \alpha_C \approx 0.050 \;\text{(on log-log, straight lines)}$$

Hoffmann et al. (2022, the "Chinchilla" paper) refined this further by jointly optimising parameter count and token count under a fixed compute budget. They found that most large models at the time were significantly undertrained — GPT-3 (175B parameters) was trained on 300B tokens when the optimal ratio for that compute budget was roughly 1:1 parameters-to-tokens. The Chinchilla model (70B parameters, 1.4T tokens) matched or outperformed GPT-3 at a fraction of the parameter count. This shifted the field toward training smaller models on more data — a change visible in LLaMA (7B–65B trained on 1T+ tokens) and its descendants.

🔤
Tokenisation
Modern Transformers use subword tokenisation (BPE, SentencePiece, tiktoken) rather than word or character tokens. This balances vocabulary size against sequence length — common words are single tokens, rare words are split into subword pieces. GPT-4 uses ~100K tokens; BERT uses ~30K.
⚡
Flash Attention
The QKᵀ matrix is O(T²) in memory — prohibitive for T=100K tokens. Flash Attention (Dao et al. 2022) computes the same result using IO-aware tiling, never materialising the full T×T matrix. Enables 10–100× longer context windows with no approximation and the same output.
🧩
Instruction Tuning (RLHF)
Pre-trained GPT models generate text but don't follow instructions reliably. RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) fine-tunes the model to produce outputs that humans rate as helpful and harmless, turning a language model into a useful assistant. InstructGPT, ChatGPT, and Claude all use variants of this.
🔀
Mixture of Experts (MoE)
Instead of activating all FFN parameters for every token, MoE routes each token through only k of N expert FFN sub-networks (e.g. top-2 of 64). This gives a model with 8× more parameters at 2× the compute cost — allowing trillion-parameter models without proportional compute scaling. GPT-4 and Mixtral use MoE.

What You Now Understand

Working backwards through all four pages of Module IV, you can now trace a complete intellectual lineage. Vanilla RNNs introduced the idea of processing sequences with a shared hidden state, but suffered from vanishing gradients and no long-range memory. LSTMs solved this with gated memory cells — additive updates through a cell state creating a gradient highway. Attention solved the encoder-decoder information bottleneck — instead of a single fixed context vector, the decoder queries all encoder states dynamically at every step. And the Transformer completed the arc by making attention the primary computational mechanism, discarding recurrence entirely to achieve full parallelism, O(1) path length between any two tokens, and the scaling properties that turned it into the foundation of modern AI.

Every major language model released in the last seven years — BERT, GPT-2, GPT-3, GPT-4, T5, LLaMA, Mistral, Gemma, Claude — is a Transformer. The core architecture has remained surprisingly stable even as scale, data, and training recipes have evolved dramatically. The components you built from scratch in this page — scaled dot-product attention, multi-head attention, positional encoding, encoder blocks with residuals and LayerNorm, masked decoder blocks with cross-attention — are the same components running inside every production language model being used today.

🚀 Open Questions — What You Might Work On
Despite the Transformer's success, fundamental questions remain open. The O(T²) attention complexity is still a hard limit for very long sequences — efficient attention variants are an active area with no clear winner. It is not understood why scaling works so reliably, or whether scaling laws will eventually break down. The mechanism behind in-context learning — how a frozen GPT-3 performs new tasks from a few examples without gradient updates — is not well understood. Mechanistic interpretability, the effort to reverse-engineer what specific circuits inside trained Transformers do, is a growing field. And the question of whether scale alone can produce reasoning, or whether architectural innovations are still needed, is actively debated. Each of these is a tractable research direction for someone who understands the architecture you just built.
